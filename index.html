Comprehensive Case Summary

I. Employment History & Legal Framework

Continuous Service & Statutory Protection

Mr George Kirby commenced employment with ASDA Stores Limited on 14 November 2000. As of the dismissal date (1 October 2025), he had accumulated 24 years 9 months of continuous service. This far exceeds the statutory 2-year minimum required for unfair dismissal protection under the Employment Rights Act 1996, s.94. By virtue of this length of service, Mr Kirby held substantial statutory employment protection.

Throughout his 24-year employment, Mr Kirby progressed to the role of Customer Trading Manager (DTM Grade) at the ASDA Tamworth store (Bitterscote Retail Park). His final salary was ¬£36,731.68 per annum with an ASDA employer pension contribution of 7% (¬£2,571.22 per annum). These figures are documented in official payslips and pension correspondence verified through the Subject Access Request.

Notice Period Entitlement

Mr Kirby's employment contract (dated 21 April 2024) specified a notice period of 12 weeks (1 week per year of service, maximum 12 weeks). This contractual entitlement reflects his extensive tenure. Under this contract, both employer and employee were required to provide 12 weeks' notice of termination. Any dismissal without proper notice or without following fair procedures would constitute a breach of contract and potential unfair dismissal.

II. Court-Ordered Parental Custody - Foundation of Sex Discrimination

Legal Status of Court Order

On 8 February 2021, Mr Kirby obtained a legally binding court order from Birmingham Family Court (Case Reference: BM20P08534) establishing shared parental custody of his 6-year-old child (George Matthew Adcock, DOB 28 September 2019). This court order is not discretionary guidance‚Äîit is enforceable law carrying criminal penalties for breach.

Custody Terms (Binding & Enforceable):

  • Term-time custody: Tuesday after school through Thursday before school (2.5 days per week during school terms)
  • School holiday custody: Equal division of all holiday periods throughout the year
  • Relocation prohibition: The order EXPLICITLY prohibits relocation of the child without further court consent

This court order creates legally protected parental status. Mr Kirby cannot legally relocate without returning to court for permission. This is precisely the same legal position as a mother with identical custody arrangements.

Relevance to Employment

Employment law recognizes that court-ordered parental custody creates material personal circumstances affecting employee's ability to work. An employer that offers an employee a role requiring relocation (20 miles in this case) without inquiring whether the employee's parental obligations permit relocation is acting unreasonably and potentially discriminatorily.

Critically, employers routinely recognize this principle when dealing with mothers. A female employee with this identical court order would never be offered a 20-mile relocation role without EXTENSIVE consultation on childcare arrangements, school pickups, holiday periods, and feasibility of compliance with the court order.

The fact that ASDA failed to make this same inquiry for a male employee with identical parental obligations constitutes less favorable treatment based on sex = direct discrimination under the Equality Act 2010, s.13.

III. The Dismissal Event - 19 July 2025

Procedural Failures (5 Independent ACAS Breaches)

Breach #1 - No Advance Notice: On 09 July 2025, Mr Kirby was called to an unscheduled meeting without prior notice. He received no indication of the meeting's purpose or subject matter. This violates the ACAS Code of Practice requirement that employees be given advance notice of meetings concerning potential dismissal or serious disciplinary matters.

Breach #2 - No Right to Representation: At no point during the meeting was Mr Kirby informed of his right to bring a companion or representative (statutory right under the Employment Relations Act 1999, s.10). The meeting minutes later disclosed that managers had been "guided not to allow representation, keep meetings under 5 minutes, avoid discussions, remain emotionless"‚Äîproving deliberate procedural circumvention.

Breach #3 - No Investigation: No investigation was conducted into whether the role elimination was genuine, whether the role could be retained in the current location, or what alternatives might exist (flexible working, home working, phased transition, different roles). This violates basic principles of fair procedure and the ACAS Code requirement for investigation before dismissal.

Breach #4 - No Written Record: Although the meeting lasted approximately 1-2 hours (indicating extensive discussion), no written record was provided to Mr Kirby. The ACAS Code requires that employees receive written confirmation of what was discussed and decided. This failure prevents Mr Kirby from verifying what was said and denies him documentation for potential dispute resolution.

Breach #5 - Grievance Rejected Without Investigation: When Mr Kirby submitted a formal grievance on 5-6 August 2025 citing 7 separate points, ASDA rejected it on the same day (6 August 2025). No investigation is possible in 24 hours. This demonstrates ASDA's predetermined position and bad faith approach, violating its own grievance policy and the ACAS Code requirement for proper investigation.

The Role Offer - Objectively Unsuitable

During the dismissal meeting, ASDA offered Mr Kirby a position as Fresh Manager at Great Bridge (approximately 20 miles from his current Tamworth location). This role was offered as a putative "suitable alternative role"‚Äîbut it was objectively unsuitable for multiple reasons:

  • Relocation Incompatible with Court Order: The 20-mile relocation is directly incompatible with the court order requiring equal school holiday custody and term-time mid-week custody. A 20-mile commute would make compliance with school pickup/dropoff obligations and holiday periods impossible.
  • Licensing Requirements: The Fresh Manager role may require specific driving qualifications or licenses that Mr Kirby does not hold (this is disputed but important if ASDA claims driving was required).
  • Grade/Demotion Issues: The role represents a potential change in grade or responsibility from his existing Customer Trading Manager position, possibly constituting a demotion or material change in terms and conditions.
  • No Transition Period: The role was offered with immediate commencement (the next day). No flexibility, no transition period, no opportunity to arrange childcare or make other accommodations.
  • Ultimatum Framing: The offer was explicitly framed as an ultimatum: accept the role or be treated as resigned/dismissed. This created psychological and contractual coercion.

Awareness of Court Order - Critical Evidence

During the dismissal meeting, Mr Kirby IMMEDIATELY objected to the role offer and explained that he could not accept it due to his court-ordered custody arrangements. The meeting minutes document that David McLawrence (the GSM conducting the meeting) ACKNOWLEDGED that he understood the constraint.

Despite this acknowledgment, ASDA proceeded with the role offer and 12-week notice period without exploring ANY solutions or alternatives. This demonstrates that ASDA was aware of the legal conflict and proceeded deliberately anyway. This is critical evidence of bad faith conduct and intentional disregard for Mr Kirby's parental obligations.

IV. Sex Discrimination Analysis - The Comparator Test

Direct Discrimination Legal Test (Equality Act 2010, s.13)

Direct discrimination occurs when a person is treated less favorably because of a protected characteristic (in this case, sex) than a person of the opposite sex would be treated in comparable circumstances.

The legal test requires identification of a "comparator"‚Äîa hypothetical person of the opposite sex in comparable circumstances. In this case, the comparator is: a female employee with the identical court-ordered shared custody of a 6-year-old child, holding an identical Customer Trading Manager role at a location suitable for that custody arrangement.

How ASDA Would Have Treated a Female Comparator

Reasonable Employer Conduct with Female Employee: If a female Customer Trading Manager with identical court-ordered shared custody received the same notice of role elimination, a reasonable employer would:

  • Inquire extensively about her parental custody arrangements and legal constraints
  • Recognize that a 20-mile relocation is incompatible with court-ordered term-time and holiday custody
  • Explore alternatives: flexible working, home working, different roles at existing location, phased transition, financial support for childcare
  • Consult on feasibility and discuss how she could comply with both work and court order obligations
  • Provide extended notice and transition period
  • Take the matter seriously rather than present as an ultimatum

This approach is considered obviously necessary and self-evident when dealing with mothers. Employers routinely acknowledge that mothers with court-ordered custody cannot be relocated without extensive discussion and planning.

How ASDA Actually Treated Mr Kirby (The Male Comparator)

Mr Kirby, despite identical court-ordered custody, received:

  • ZERO inquiry into his parental obligations or court order
  • NO consultation on feasibility of relocation + custody compliance
  • A 20-mile relocation role with IMMEDIATE commencement demanded
  • An ultimatum: accept or be dismissed
  • Refusal treated as resignation (dismissal consequence)

When Mr Kirby objected and explained the court order, the manager acknowledged understanding the constraint but proceeded anyway. This demonstrates conscious disregard for parental obligations‚Äîconduct that would be unthinkable if dealing with a mother with identical circumstances.

Why This Constitutes Sex Discrimination

By failing to inquire into and accommodate Mr Kirby's court-ordered parental custody in a way that would be automatic for a female employee with identical circumstances, ASDA:

  • Treated Mr Kirby less favorably than a female comparator would be treated
  • Based this less favorable treatment on sex (because the protective inquiry would be mandatory for a mother)
  • Caused detriment (dismissal as a result of unsuitable role offer)

This is textbook direct discrimination. Modern tribunal authorities recognize that male parental rights are equally protected as female parental rights. Court-ordered shared custody creates identical legal obligations and protections regardless of the employee's sex.
